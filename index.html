What We Make

Phthalic Anhydride

Phthalic Anhydride (PA) is one of the world's highest-volume industrial chemicals. It is the starting material for phthalate plasticisers -- the compounds that make PVC flexible -- and for unsaturated polyester resins, alkyd coatings, and dozens of specialty intermediates.

Without PA, there are no flexible PVC pipes for water supply. No protective coatings for bridges and infrastructure. No phthalate-free plasticisers for food-grade packaging. PA is not a niche -- it is foundational.

IGPL produces PA at a scale that anchors India's entire supply chain. When domestic demand rises -- in construction, automotive, infrastructure -- IGPL's output is the first point of supply.

Why IGPL

Defensible Position

Strategic Location

Located at Taloja, in Maharashtra's chemical corridor. 50 km from JNPT port -- India's largest container gateway. Close proximity to domestic O-xylene suppliers and major downstream customers in Maharashtra and Gujarat.

Unique Position

Largest single-site PA complex in India. Five dedicated PA plants on one campus. ~50% share of India's PA market. This concentration is difficult and capital-intensive to replicate -- it took three decades.

High Utilization

IGPL runs at high capacity utilization through a combination of annual contracts with Indian customers and spot export sales. Steam recovery systems reduce energy costs. Revenue per tonne is maximised through byproduct valorisation (Maleic Anhydride, Benzoic Acid) and downstream integration (Diethyl Phthalate).

Technology

Von-Heyden Low Energy Process

IGPL uses Wacker Chemie GmbH's Von-Heyden Low Energy Process for PA production -- one of the most energy-efficient vapour-phase oxidation routes available globally.

The process uses a fixed-bed, vanadium-based catalyst system that delivers consistent product quality (PA purity >=99.8%) while minimising energy consumption per tonne. This translates to lower variable costs and a smaller environmental footprint.

IGPL's operational team has optimised this process over three decades, with continuous debottlenecking and incremental capacity additions across five plant expansions.
